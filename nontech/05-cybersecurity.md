# Cybersecurity

## What

Cybersecurity threats affect every business, regardless of size. This covers the most common threats and what every business person should know to protect their organization.

## Common Threats

### Phishing

An attacker sends an email pretending to be someone trustworthy (your bank, your CEO, a vendor) to trick you into clicking a link, opening an attachment, or providing credentials.

How to spot it:
- Unexpected urgency ("Your account will be closed in 24 hours")
- Slightly wrong sender address (support@y0urbank.com instead of yourbank.com)
- Requests for passwords, credit card numbers, or wire transfers
- Generic greetings ("Dear Customer" instead of your name)

What to do: Don't click. Verify through a separate channel. Call the person directly. When in doubt, report it to your IT team.

### Ransomware

Malicious software that encrypts your files and demands payment to unlock them.

How it gets in:
- Phishing emails with infected attachments
- Visiting compromised websites
- Exploiting unpatched software vulnerabilities

Impact:
- Business operations halt
- Data becomes inaccessible
- Recovery can take days or weeks, even with backups
- Paying the ransom doesn't guarantee data recovery

Prevention:
- Keep software updated (patches fix vulnerabilities)
- Back up data regularly and test restoration
- Train employees to recognize phishing
- Use endpoint protection software

### Data Breaches

Unauthorized access to sensitive data. Could be external attackers or insider threats.

What gets stolen:
- Customer personal information
- Financial data
- Intellectual property
- Employee records

Consequences:
- Regulatory fines (GDPR fines can reach 4% of annual revenue)
- Legal liability
- Reputation damage
- Customer loss

### Social Engineering

Manipulating people into breaking security procedures. Not a technology problem — a human one.

Examples:
- Someone calls claiming to be IT support and asks for your password
- A visitor tailgates through a secure door behind an employee
- An email from "the CEO" asks you to wire money urgently

Defense: Verify identity through established channels. No legitimate IT support will ask for your password.

## What Every Business Should Do

### The Basics

1. **Use strong, unique passwords** — Use a password manager. One password per account.
2. **Enable multi-factor authentication** — Something you know (password) + something you have (phone). Stops most account compromise.
3. **Keep software updated** — Patches fix known vulnerabilities. Delaying patches is leaving the door open.
4. **Back up data regularly** — Test that backups work. A backup you can't restore is not a backup.
5. **Train employees** — People are the weakest link. Regular training reduces risk significantly.

### For Leadership

- Allocate budget for security tools and training
- Have an incident response plan — know who to call and what to do before an incident happens
- Conduct regular security reviews
- Understand your regulatory obligations (GDPR, HIPAA, PCI, SOX)
- Treat security as an ongoing practice, not a one-time project

## The Reality

- It's not "if" you'll face a security incident, it's "when"
- Small businesses are targeted more often because they typically have less security
- Most breaches are caused by human error, not sophisticated hacking
- Recovery is always more expensive than prevention
