# AI for Business

## What

AI is being sold as a solution to almost every business problem. This guide helps you evaluate AI claims, understand what AI can realistically do, and make informed decisions.

## What AI Actually Does

AI finds patterns in large amounts of data and uses those patterns to make predictions or generate content.

Real capabilities:
- Automate repetitive, rules-based tasks
- Analyze large datasets to find trends
- Generate drafts of documents, emails, and reports
- Answer questions based on provided information
- Classify and categorize information

What AI does not do:
- Understand context the way humans do
- Make judgment calls involving ethics or nuance
- Replace domain expertise
- Guarantee accuracy

## Evaluating Vendor Claims

When a vendor says their product uses AI, ask these questions:

### "What specific problem does the AI solve?"

If the answer is vague ("it makes everything better"), that's a red flag. Specific problems have specific solutions.

### "What data was the AI trained on?"

AI is only as good as its training data. If it was trained on data from a different industry or context, its output may not be relevant to your business.

### "How accurate is it? How do you measure that?"

"Very accurate" is not an answer. "95% precision on our test set of 10,000 examples" is. Ask for specific metrics.

### "What happens when it's wrong?"

Every AI makes mistakes. The question isn't whether it will be wrong, but what happens when it is. Is there a human in the loop? Can you override it?

### "How much human oversight is needed?"

If the vendor says "none," they're either lying or selling something very simple. Most useful AI requires some human involvement.

### "What are the privacy and data security implications?"

Where does your data go? Is it used to train their models? What compliance certifications do they have?

## Realistic Expectations

### AI Will

- Reduce time spent on routine tasks by 20-50%
- Improve consistency of output
- Enable your team to focus on higher-value work
- Require investment in training and process changes
- Produce occasional errors that need human correction

### AI Will Not

- Replace the need for skilled employees
- Eliminate all errors or inefficiencies
- Work perfectly out of the box with zero configuration
- Solve problems you haven't clearly defined
- Deliver ROI without a plan for how to use it

## How to Start

1. Identify a specific, measurable problem
2. Find an AI tool that addresses that problem
3. Run a small pilot with clear success criteria
4. Measure results against the criteria
5. Scale if results are positive, stop if they aren't

## Common Pitfalls

- Buying AI because competitors are using it. Your problems and data are different.
- Expecting AI to solve process problems. If your process is broken, AI will automate the brokenness.
- Not investing in training. AI tools are only useful if people know how to use them effectively.
- Ignoring data quality. AI trained on messy data produces messy results.
- Not measuring ROI. Without measurement, you can't tell if the AI investment is paying off.
